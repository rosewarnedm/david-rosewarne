Profile

I am a consultant radiologist at New Cross Hospital, Wolverhampton, where I have worked since 2009. My clinical practice covers the full general radiology service — CT, MRI, ultrasound, fluoroscopy, and plain film reporting — with subspecialty interests in chest imaging, head and neck, cardiac CT, and image-guided biopsy and drainage. I have a particular interest in interstitial lung disease and, more recently, lung cancer screening through the Targeted Lung Health Check programme.

My background is unusual for a radiologist. I read Natural Sciences at Cambridge, then completed a PhD in quantum optics at the University of London, before working for two years as an analyst at Logica on machine vision and signal processing projects for UK Government departments. I subsequently retrained in medicine at Birmingham, qualifying with honours in 1999.

Since 2020 I have developed a parallel strand of work in digital medicine, applying natural language processing, large language models, and clinical AI to problems in radiology audit, education, and service improvement. I held an NHS England Topol Fellowship (2022–23) and was Radiology Lead for the West Midlands Clinical Research Network (NIHR, 2022–24). I am a Visiting Researcher at the University of Surrey, working on human-AI cooperation in breast imaging.

Programme Description

The CLEAR Programme

CLEAR is a multicentre, multi-institution initiative to develop and validate large language model pipelines for the systematic audit of NHS radiology report corpora at scale. I am clinical lead for the programme, having led the advocacy for multicentre collaboration and the national meeting that brought together imaging network partners. The programme operates through a trusted research environment provided by 33n, which enables the compliant processing of identifiable health data across participating institutions.

The central hypothesis is that LLM-based audit, applied at scale across a network of NHS departments, can identify variation in radiological practice that escapes conventional point-in-time audit. Structured feedback to departments is expected to reduce unwarranted variation, improve reporting standardisation, and in the case of CTPA and CT brain, reduce unnecessary downstream imaging and its associated cost and radiation burden.

The three workstreams of the programme are:

1
CT pulmonary angiography yield
LLM classification of CTPA reports at scale to measure positivity rates, identify departments with outlying yield, and examine the relationship between clinical indication quality and diagnostic output.
2
CT brain follow-up recommendation rates
Automated extraction and analysis of follow-up recommendations from acute CT brain reports to quantify recommendation burden, identify variation across reporters and departments, and support efforts to reduce low-value imaging.
3
Detection of unwarranted variation across imaging networks
Cross-institutional comparison of reporting patterns and clinical language using LLM embeddings and classification, with the aim of identifying systematic differences in practice that cannot be attributed to case mix.

The immediate objective is multicentre validation of pipelines developed in the single-centre NHSE-funded phase. Three posters from the single-centre work were presented at the European Congress of Radiology 2025.

Radiology AI Strategy — New Cross Hospital / Royal Wolverhampton NHS Trust

Dr David Rosewarne, Consultant Radiologist & Digital Lead  ·  2025–2028

Prepared for the RCR AI Strategy Meeting. Restricted circulation.

1. Context and Rationale

Radiology is among the clinical specialties most immediately affected by the deployment of large-scale AI. The department at New Cross Hospital serves a population of approximately 320,000 and handles over 300,000 imaging examinations annually. Against a background of sustained reporting demand growth and constrained consultant numbers, AI presents both an operational necessity and a strategic opportunity.

This strategy describes the department's current AI programme, its three-year objectives, and the governance framework required to deploy AI safely, evaluate it rigorously, and contribute to the evidence base through publication and multicentre collaboration. It does not treat AI as a procurement exercise. The department's approach is grounded in scientific evaluation, data-driven audit, and the principle that clinical AI deployment without robust outcome measurement is professionally indefensible.

"The question is not whether AI will change radiology practice, but whether radiologists will be in a position to evaluate, govern, and improve those changes — or merely receive them."

2. Current Digital Programme

The department is currently running or contributing to the following AI and digital initiatives:

  • Clinical AI triage (harrison.ai): Implementation underway for lung cancer pathway triage and acute CT brain prioritisation. Expected to reduce time-to-report for urgent findings and support capacity management.
  • CLEAR Programme: Multicentre LLM audit programme (clinical lead: Rosewarne). Three NHSE-sponsored workstreams auditing CTPA yield, CT brain recommendation rates, and unwarranted variation at scale. Partnership with 33n for trusted multicentre data processing.
  • Auto-TeaCH: NLP application for harvesting teaching cases from departmental report archives. Deployed via 33n infrastructure. In evaluation at multiple radiology academies.
  • REALM: LLM-based error detection pipeline for acute radiology, supporting departmental quality improvement and targeted educational feedback.
  • Lung cancer screening (TLHC): Reporting low-dose CT since May 2025 with automated audit infrastructure developed for the forthcoming Wolverhampton population screening launch.
  • PecMan (University of Surrey): Contributing to research on human-AI cooperation and algorithmic fairness in mammogram interpretation.

3. Strategic Objectives (2025–2028)

The department's AI strategy is organised around four objectives:

  • Clinical safety and outcome improvement: Deploy AI tools with measurable patient benefit — reduced time to diagnosis for urgent pathology, improved sensitivity for lung cancer and pulmonary embolism, reduction in low-value follow-up imaging.
  • Operational efficiency and standardisation: Use LLM-based audit to identify and reduce unwarranted variation in reporting practice, with the CLEAR Programme providing the multicentre infrastructure for this work.
  • Education and workforce development: Integrate AI-assisted case selection (Auto-TeaCH, REALM) into trainee education, and build departmental competency in evaluating AI tools critically.
  • Research and publication: Contribute to the national evidence base through multicentre studies, conference presentations, and peer-reviewed publication.

4. Governance Framework

All AI deployments in the department operate within a governance framework covering clinical oversight, data management, and post-deployment evaluation.

  • Data governance: Multicentre data processing for CLEAR and related projects is conducted within the 33n Trusted Research Environment, which provides ISO-27001 compliant infrastructure for the handling of identifiable NHS data and satisfies the requirements of NHS DSPT and GDPR.
  • Clinical oversight: AI outputs in clinical pathways are reviewed by consultant radiologists. No AI system operates in an unsupervised reporting capacity. All deployments include a defined escalation pathway.
  • AI as a Medical Device (AIaMD): Commercial deployments (harrison.ai, Annalise.ai) are UKCA-marked and evaluated against the MHRA's Software and AI as a Medical Device guidance. In-house tools (CLEAR pipelines, Auto-TeaCH, REALM) are classified as service improvement software and evaluated under the department's clinical audit and QI governance.
  • Fairness and bias: Work through the PecMan collaboration at the University of Surrey is informing our approach to algorithmic fairness evaluation in deployed AI systems, particularly with respect to demographic subgroup performance.

5. Workforce and Culture

The department's approach to AI adoption is informed by the principle that radiologists who understand the technical basis of AI tools are better placed to evaluate, govern, and improve them than those who do not. This is the core argument of The DigitalElf Project, which provides a model for bottom-up clinician engagement with digital medicine.

Specific workforce development activities include:

  • Structured introduction to AI evaluation methods for all radiology trainees, integrated into the departmental teaching programme.
  • Engagement with the West Midlands Imaging Academy on AI-assisted case selection and portfolio summarisation tools.
  • Participation in the DetectedX platform for perception training and AI-assisted case review.
  • Internal dissemination of CLEAR Programme methodology to build departmental familiarity with LLM-based audit.

7. Risks and Mitigations

RiskMitigation
AI tool underperforms in local population (case mix, scanner variation) Prospective local validation before clinical integration; ongoing performance monitoring via CLEAR audit infrastructure
Over-reliance on AI outputs by junior staff Defined supervision requirements; AI outputs labelled as decision support only; trainee education programme
Data governance failure in multicentre CLEAR work 33n TRE provides contractual and technical safeguards; DPIA completed for each workstream
Loss of clinical AI expertise if key individuals leave DigitalElf model for distributed capability; documentation and open-source tooling reduces key-person dependency
Regulatory change (AIaMD classification) Monitoring MHRA and NHSX guidance; legal advice sought for in-house tool classification
